I. Comprehensive Theory

Principle: The experiment is based on the phenomenon of Fraunhofer Diffraction. When a monochromatic, coherent beam of light from a Laser source strikes a diffraction grating (a glass plate with several thousand parallel lines per inch), the light is diffracted. According to Huygens' Principle, each slit acts as a source of secondary wavelets.

The Grating Equation: Constructive interference occurs when the path difference between light rays from adjacent slits is an integral multiple of the wavelength. This is mathematically expressed as:

$$n\lambda = d \sin \theta_n$$

Key Parameters:

  • Grating Constant (\(d\)): The distance between two successive slits. If \(N\) is the number of lines per inch, then \(d = \frac{2.54}{N} \text{ cm}\).
  • Order of Diffraction (\(n\)): The integer representing the bright spot sequence (e.g., 1st, 2nd order).
  • Diffraction Angle (\(\theta_n\)): The angle at which the \(n^{th}\) order beam is deviated from the original path.
  • Small Angle Approximation: In this setup, \(\sin \theta_n \approx \tan \theta_n = \frac{X_m}{D}\), where \(X_m\) is the distance of the spot from the center and \(D\) is the distance between grating and screen.

II. Experimental Procedure

  1. Alignment: Place the Laser source and the diffraction grating on the optical bench. Ensure the Laser beam hits the grating perpendicularly (normal incidence).
  2. Setup Geometry: Adjust the Distance \(D\) (Grating to Screen) using the slider provided in the control panel. Start with a distance of 20 cm.
  3. Observation: Switch on the Laser. You will observe a series of bright spots on the screen. The central spot is the zero-order (\(n=0\)).
  4. Measurement: For each order (\(n=1, 2, 3\)), measure the distance of the spot to the Right (\(R\)) and Left (\(L\)) of the central maximum using the screen scale.
  5. Data Entry: Enter the measured \(R\) and \(L\) values into the Observation Table. The system will automatically compute the mean distance \(X_m\).
  6. Calculation: The tool will determine the diffraction angle \(\theta_n\) and finally calculate the wavelength \(\lambda\) in Angstrom units (Å).
  7. Repetition: Repeat the steps for different distances of \(D\) (e.g., 40 cm and 60 cm) to ensure the precision of your results.
